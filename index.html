🎯 What is This?

In 1787, Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart created an ingenious system that lets anyone compose waltzes without knowing music theory. You simply roll two dice 16 times, look up the results in his special number tables (Zahlentafel), and play the corresponding pre-composed musical measures. Each game creates a completely unique waltz!

🎲 How the Dice Game Works

Step 1: Understanding the Structure

A waltz consists of 16 measures (musical bars), divided into two halves:

  • First Half: Measures 1-8
  • Second Half: Measures 9-16

Step 2: Roll the Dice

For each of the 16 positions:

  1. Roll two six-sided dice
  2. Add the results (sum will be 2-12)
  3. Look up the dice sum in the appropriate table
  4. Find the measure number for that column position

Step 3: Build Your Waltz

Example:

Roll for Position 1 (Column A): 🎲 3 + 🎲 4 = 7

Look up row 7, column A in the First Half table → Measure 104

This measure becomes the first measure of your waltz!

Step 4: Repeat

Continue rolling for all 16 positions (8 columns × 2 halves) to complete your unique waltz.

Mozart's Genius

Mozart pre-composed 176 different musical measures, carefully designed so that any combination would sound harmonious. The measures were arranged in his tables so that:

  • Each column position works with any dice roll
  • Musical phrases flow naturally from one to the next
  • The overall structure maintains proper waltz form
  • Harmonic progressions remain pleasing regardless of combination

🎻 Historical Background

The Original Musikalisches Würfelspiel

Mozart's Musikalisches Würfelspiel (Musical Dice Game), catalogued as K.516f, represents one of the earliest examples of algorithmic composition in Western music history. Created around 1787, this ingenious system demonstrates how mathematical principles can be applied to create art, predating modern computational music by over 200 years.

The Historical Manuscript

The original consists of:

  • Two number tables (Zahlentafel) mapping dice rolls to measure numbers
  • 176 pre-composed measures of waltz music in 3/4 time
  • Simple instructions for combining them into complete pieces

Context in Mozart's Life

In 1787, Mozart was at the height of his creative powers. This was the same year he composed:

  • Don Giovanni (K.527)
  • Eine kleine Nachtmusik (K.525)
  • Several string quintets and other masterworks

The dice game represented both a playful diversion and a serious exploration of musical structure and variation.

The Tradition of Musical Games

Mozart wasn't alone in creating musical dice games. The 18th century saw several composers experiment with aleatory (chance-based) composition, including:

  • C.P.E. Bach - Created similar dice games
  • Joseph Haydn - Experimented with chance elements
  • Johann Philipp Kirnberger - Published "The Ever-Ready Polonaise and Minuet Composer" (1757)

Modern Influence

Mozart's dice game influenced:

  • 20th century aleatoric music (John Cage, Karlheinz Stockhausen)
  • Generative music systems
  • Computer-aided composition
  • Modern procedural music generation in video games

Authenticity Questions

Note: While traditionally attributed to Mozart, some musicologists debate the authorship of K.516f. The piece was first published in 1793, two years after Mozart's death, and the manuscript's attribution has been questioned. However, the style and sophistication of the system strongly suggest Mozart's involvement, and it remains widely accepted as his work.

Understanding the Tables:
